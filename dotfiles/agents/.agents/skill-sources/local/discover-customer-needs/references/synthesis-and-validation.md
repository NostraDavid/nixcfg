# Synthesis and Validation

Synthesis turns heterogeneous observations into decision support without
laundering assumptions into facts. Preserve traceability and disagreement
throughout.

## Evidence Ledger

Maintain one row per material observation or claim:

| Field            | Content                                                                          |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| ID               | Stable local identifier.                                                         |
| Segment and role | Whose context the evidence represents.                                           |
| Situation        | Trigger, environment, workflow stage, and recency.                               |
| Observation      | What happened or what artifact shows, without interpretation.                    |
| Source           | Interview, observation, log, document, system, workshop, or experiment.          |
| Strength         | Direct, corroborated, specific self-report, generic self-report, or speculation. |
| Consequence      | Time, money, risk, delay, rework, emotion, safety, or opportunity.               |
| Interpretation   | What the team thinks the observation means.                                      |
| Contradiction    | Evidence or perspective that does not fit.                                       |
| Decision link    | Which unknown, need, model element, or decision it informs.                      |

Do not store personal identifiers unless necessary. Link to controlled source
material instead of copying sensitive content into the ledger.

## Analysis Discipline

Keep analysis and synthesis distinct even though they alternate. Analysis breaks
an interview, observation, or artifact into source-linked incidents, stories,
terms, and claims. Synthesis combines those elements into patterns,
explanations, implications, and opportunities.

Debriefs and field highlights are early analysis, not findings. After the batch,
write a short topline of emerging themes and questions, explicitly marked
preliminary. Then review the underlying material, tag meaningful passages or
incidents, and bring source-identified elements together for collaborative
clustering. Let categories change as cases accumulate instead of forcing every
observation into the first scheme.

Separate the evidence presentation from the response workshop. First help the
team understand the finding and its implication; then generate possible
responses in a different activity. Phrase an opportunity broadly enough to
permit competing solutions and trace each candidate back to the need it
addresses.

## Derive Needs

Group evidence by segment, situation, desired outcome, and causal pattern. A
defensible need states:

- who encounters it and in what situation;
- what outcome or progress is required;
- what currently prevents or degrades that outcome;
- why the consequence matters;
- which observations support it and which contradict it.

Keep the solution out of the need statement. "Needs a dashboard" is usually a
proposed capability; "cannot detect failed settlements before the daily cutoff"
describes an outcome gap. Preserve the dashboard as a candidate opportunity
linked to that gap.

Separate:

- business objective: measurable organizational change;
- customer or user need: desired progress in a situation;
- task or scenario: work performed to reach a goal;
- functional behavior: response the solution must provide;
- quality expectation: how well behavior must work in a stated context;
- business rule: policy or calculation independent of the chosen interface;
- constraint: externally binding limit on solution or project;
- idea: one possible way to meet a need.

## Compare and Weight

- Analyze coherent segments separately; an average can describe nobody.
- Seek source diversity, not only participant count. Five colleagues repeating
  one policy are not five independent observations.
- Look for negative cases: people who do not experience the problem, abandoned
  workarounds, and situations where the supposed consequence does not occur.
- Record incentives and power. A buyer's priority, an operator's burden, and an
  end user's behavior can all be true and incompatible.
- Prefer repeated behavior and contemporaneous artifacts, but investigate
  meaningful anomalies rather than discarding them.
- Express confidence qualitatively and explain why. Do not invent statistical
  certainty from a small, purposive sample.

## Coverage Review

For the in-scope work, check whether discovery has addressed:

- purpose, business outcome, success measure, and decision owner;
- target segments, user classes, buyers, operators, approvers, and affected
  non-users;
- present workflow, trigger, normal path, alternatives, exceptions, and
  completion signal;
- frequency, volume, timing, location, seasonality, and concurrency where
  material;
- pain, consequence, workaround, current effort or spend, and switching cost;
- events, actors, handoffs, responsibilities, decisions, and business rules;
- information inputs, outputs, ownership, lifecycle, retention, and
  sensitivities;
- adjacent systems, interfaces, manual operations, and solution boundary;
- quality trade-offs, failure behavior, recovery, support, operations, and
  adoption;
- legal, policy, accessibility, privacy, security, safety, and organizational
  constraints;
- assumptions, contradictions, unresolved decisions, and disconfirming evidence.

Coverage is risk-based. Do not force irrelevant categories into a simple
discovery.

## Validate

Choose the cheapest test that could reveal a consequential error:

- replay a real scenario against the current workflow or model;
- ask another user class to correct the model;
- inspect the cited artifact or system record;
- test an alternative, exception, or failure path;
- convert a policy into examples or a decision table;
- convert a quality adjective into a measurable scenario;
- use a low-fidelity prototype for comprehension or workflow;
- use a technical spike for feasibility, integration, or performance
  uncertainty;
- run a concierge, pilot, or commitment test when real behavior is needed.

Validation questions should invite correction: "What is wrong or missing?",
"When would this not be true?", and "Show me a case that follows a different
path." Avoid asking only whether the summary looks good.

## Discovery Brief

Write the answer before the chronology of research:

1. **Decision**: what choice this evidence informs, owner, deadline, and scope.
2. **Current understanding**: concise problem, outcomes, target segments, and
   workflow.
3. **Needs**: evidence-backed need statements with confidence and contradictory
   evidence.
4. **Supporting contracts**: rules, data, interfaces, quality expectations, and
   constraints as relevant.
5. **Evidence**: strongest observations, source diversity, and notable negative
   cases.
6. **Opportunities**: candidate responses clearly labeled as options, not
   requirements, and linked to the findings that motivated them.
7. **Unknowns and risks**: what could still reverse or materially change the
   decision.
8. **Next learning step**: smallest activity, owner, and stopping condition.
9. **Traceability**: need and conclusion IDs linked to ledger entries, models,
   or controlled artifacts.

Do not imply consensus because stakeholders attended a session. Name unresolved
conflicts and the person empowered to decide them. Do not call discovery
complete merely because an interview quota or workshop agenda was exhausted.
