# Interviewing and Evidence

Use this reference to prepare or conduct customer and stakeholder conversations,
or to assess whether existing notes support a decision.

## Prepare

- Define at most three learning goals for a batch of conversations.
- Write the current belief and the observation that would weaken or overturn it.
- Recruit a focused, identifiable segment. Record why each participant can
  supply relevant evidence.
- Learn public and repository facts first; do not spend interview time asking
  what can be looked up.
- Decide which artifacts would clarify the work: a recent case, screen, form,
  report, spreadsheet, message, ticket, policy, invoice, calendar entry, or
  handoff.
- Plan a useful next step, but do not force a commitment when the meeting is
  exploratory or the participant lacks authority.
- Send a short purpose and topic agenda. Avoid priming participants with the
  desired answer.

## Build the Guide and Field Team

Translate the business decision into research questions, then translate each
research question into participant-friendly prompts and activities. A research
question names what the team must learn; participant questions are the
conversational means for learning it. Do not ask participants to answer the
business decision directly.

Create a flexible interview guide rather than a script:

1. introduction, consent, timing, and participant background;
2. main topics, present behavior, recent examples, artifacts, and contextual
   activities;
3. reflection and deliberately labeled speculative or ideal-world prompts, only
   when useful;
4. correction, missing topics, participant questions, next steps, and close.

Assign rough durations to sections, rehearse questions aloud, and share the
guide with the research team to expose missing topics. Expect the sequence and
wording to change in the conversation. Revise the guide after early sessions
when the evidence exposes a better path.

Prefer a field team of two: one person leads while the other listens, captures,
and notices missed threads or bias. More visible interviewers change the power
dynamic and can reduce openness. Keep additional observers out of the
interaction or provide a separate view. For remote sessions, assign a separate
person to handle access and technical failures so the lead can stay present.

Obtain informed consent before recording, photographing, collecting artifacts,
or inviting observers. Explain the intended use, storage, access, and
participant's ability to decline. Collect only what the research decision
requires.

## Open and Anchor

Briefly explain the topic, timing, and why this person's experience matters
without revealing the desired conclusion. Establish that the participant is the
expert and that correction is welcome. Make clear that the product or solution
is not being evaluated unless it truly is. Start broad, then anchor the
discussion in a specific occurrence:

- "Tell me about the last time this happened."
- "What triggered it?"
- "Walk me through what you did from there."
- "Show me the artifact you used at that step."
- "Who became involved next, and what did they need?"
- "How did you know the work was complete?"

Past-specific prompts reduce recall distortion and expose behavior, tools,
sequence, and exceptions. Observation is stronger when practical: ask the
participant to perform or reconstruct the task instead of only describing the
ideal process.

## Follow the Evidence

Use the participant's answer to choose the next question. Useful ladders
include:

- occurrence -> trigger -> steps -> actors -> artifacts -> outcome;
- pain -> consequence -> frequency -> affected party -> current response ->
  residual harm;
- workaround -> why chosen -> effort or spend -> alternatives tried -> switching
  barrier;
- request -> intended outcome -> recent example -> current method -> constraint
  -> success signal;
- decision -> criteria -> alternatives -> authority -> budget -> procurement or
  approval path;
- quality claim -> failure scenario -> acceptable degradation -> measure ->
  verification method.

Ask "why" carefully. Prefer neutral forms such as "What made that important?" or
"What happens if it is not done?" when a blunt why could sound accusatory. Allow
silence. Reflect key terms and ask the speaker to correct the interpretation.

Ask short questions and only one at a time. Use minimal, neutral
acknowledgements so enthusiasm does not teach the participant which answer is
valued. When returning from a tangent, acknowledge the last answer before
changing direction. Use the participant's language even when the team's
terminology is different; resolve the meaning later rather than correcting them
in the moment.

## Repair Weak Answers

| Weak signal                       | Repair                                                                                                     |
| --------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Compliment or approval            | Acknowledge briefly, then return to a recent example or observed behavior.                                 |
| Generic claim such as "we always" | Ask for the most recent occurrence and one counterexample.                                                 |
| Future promise                    | Ask what they have already done, spent, scheduled, or given up.                                            |
| Feature idea                      | Ask what outcome it enables, when the underlying problem last occurred, and why the current method failed. |
| Emotional language                | Ask for the concrete consequence; intensity alone does not establish impact.                               |
| Numerical rating                  | Ask what experience produced the rating and what would move it one point.                                  |
| Official process                  | Ask for a real case and where actual practice diverged.                                                    |
| Vague quality word                | Ask for a scenario, threshold, affected function, and acceptable failure behavior.                         |

Do not correct the participant, sell the idea, complete their sentences, or
defend a proposed solution. If a solution must be shown, clearly mark the
transition from problem discovery to solution evaluation. Test comprehension and
usability through tasks and observation, not through "Do you like it?"

## Judge Evidence

Use an evidence ladder rather than counting positive statements:

1. direct observation, system record, or contemporaneous artifact;
2. specific recent behavior corroborated by detail or another source;
3. existing expenditure, repeated workaround, or concrete sacrifice;
4. scheduled access, introduction, data contribution, trial, deposit, or another
   relevant commitment;
5. specific recollection from one participant;
6. general statement, preference, opinion, or future intention;
7. compliment, politeness, or agreement after a leading prompt.

Higher does not automatically mean true. Record recency, representativeness,
source incentives, contradictions, and whether the evidence concerns the same
segment and context. A commitment shows seriousness only when its cost is
meaningful to that participant.

## Capture and Close

Capture these separately during or immediately after the conversation:

- observed facts and artifacts;
- participant language and defined terms;
- tasks, triggers, actors, handoffs, exceptions, and outcomes;
- pain, workaround, frequency, consequence, spend, and constraints;
- rules, decisions, data, integrations, and quality expectations;
- interpretations and assumptions made by the interviewer;
- ideas and requests, without promoting them to needs;
- contradictions, surprises, follow-ups, and referrals.

Close by reflecting the most important learning and inviting correction: ask
what was misunderstood, what important topic was missed, and who or what could
provide a materially different perspective. State the agreed next step
precisely. Debrief with the field team immediately and before the next session
when possible: record surprises, missed questions, bias, methodological
problems, and what to change in the guide. Label quick field highlights as
preliminary; do not let them masquerade as cross-participant findings. Review
the full notes with the team and choose the next unknowns; notes that never
influence beliefs or decisions are waste.
